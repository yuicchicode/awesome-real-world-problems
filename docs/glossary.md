# Glossary

This glossary explains common terms used throughout the repository.

It is not meant to be academic.
It exists to help contributors understand the editorial and technical language used in the project.

---

## Problem statement
A clear description of a real-world pain point.

In this repository, a problem statement is not a product idea.
It describes what is hard, confusing, fragmented, slow, stressful, or unreliable in real life.

---

## Problem summary
A short explanation of the problem.

This section introduces the issue in a simple and direct way before the more detailed narrative begins.

---

## Real-world report
A first-person description of the problem, written as if someone is genuinely experiencing it.

This section gives the entry a human voice and real context.

---

## Why this is difficult
A section that explains the hidden complexity behind the problem.

This goes beyond the surface symptom and shows why the issue keeps happening in practice.

---

## Why it matters
A section that explains the impact of the problem.

This may include operational, emotional, financial, social, or strategic consequences.

---

## Open-source opportunity
A section that explains why the problem is a good fit for an open-source project.

This usually highlights shared usefulness, real-world relevance, modularity, accessibility, or collaboration potential.

---

## Technical requirements
A list of practical product or system needs that a possible solution should consider.

These requirements should guide implementation thinking without prescribing only one final solution.

---

## Suggested MVP
A realistic first version of a possible solution.

The MVP should be useful enough to matter, but small enough to feel buildable.

---

## Possible extensions
Ideas for how a project could evolve after the MVP.

These may include integrations, analytics, automation, collaboration features, AI assistance, or advanced reporting.

---

## Category
A thematic area used to group related problems.

Examples:
- healthcare
- education
- daily-life
- logistics-transport
- sales-marketing

---

## Stakeholder
Any person or group affected by the problem.

Examples:
- customer
- caregiver
- manager
- volunteer
- teacher
- patient
- store owner

---

## User role
A type of participant inside a possible solution.

Examples:
- admin
- staff member
- caregiver
- patient
- coordinator
- driver

---

## Workflow
The sequence of actions, responsibilities, and events that make a process happen.

Workflows are often messy in real life, which is why many problems in this repository exist.

---

## Visibility
The ability to clearly see what is happening in a process.

Lack of visibility is one of the most common patterns in this repository.

---

## Fragmented information
Information that exists, but is spread across too many places.

Examples:
- WhatsApp messages
- spreadsheets
- emails
- paper notes
- memory
- disconnected systems

---

## Recurring task
A task that repeats regularly.

Examples:
- weekly cleaning
- monthly medication
- recurring inventory check
- routine follow-up

---

## Follow-up
The ongoing observation or tracking of something after an initial event.

Examples:
- patient follow-up
- onboarding follow-up
- donor follow-up
- lead follow-up

---

## Coordination
The process of aligning people, information, timing, and responsibilities.

Many problems in this repository are actually coordination failures.

---

## Dependency on memory
A situation where a process only works because someone remembers what should happen.

This is a common source of fragility in real-world systems.

---

## Operational pain
A practical problem that affects execution, routine, or delivery.

Examples:
- delays
- forgotten tasks
- missing updates
- duplicated work
- weak follow-up
- unclear ownership

---

## Ownership
The person or role responsible for a task, update, decision, or outcome.

Lack of ownership clarity often creates confusion and delays.

---

## Audit trail
A record of what changed, when it changed, and who changed it.

This is important in many categories where visibility and accountability matter.

---

## Low digital maturity
A context where people or organizations do not have strong digital processes, tools, or habits yet.

Many problems in this repository happen in low-structure, low-digital-maturity environments.

---

## Shared visibility
A state where everyone involved can see the same reliable version of what is happening.

This is often one of the main goals behind possible solutions.

---

## Modular solution
A solution that can start small and grow over time.

Open-source projects often benefit from this approach.

---

## Open-source fit
How well a problem lends itself to being solved in public, collaboratively, and usefully.

A problem has strong open-source fit when it is:
- real
- recurring
- understandable
- useful to more than one context
- technically interesting
- possible to build in layers
