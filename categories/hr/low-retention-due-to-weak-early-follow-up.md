# Low retention due to failures in the first few months or lack of follow-up

## Problem summary
Another serious HR problem is low retention in the first months after hiring. The company spends time and effort to recruit, interview, hire, and onboard someone, but shortly after that the person becomes disengaged, insecure, lost, or already close to leaving.

## Real-world report
Another very serious problem in HR is low retention during the first months after hiring. The company invests time and energy to publish the role, recruit, interview, hire, and integrate a new person, but not long after that, the employee is already discouraged, insecure, confused, or even leaving the company.

In many cases, this does not happen because the person was necessarily a bad hire or because the role made no sense. It happens because the first months were handled poorly.

In practice, the beginning of a new employee’s journey is a very sensitive phase. This is the moment when they are trying to understand the company culture, the pace of the team, the expectations of the role, internal processes, tools, and even interpersonal dynamics. If there is no clear follow-up, no well-structured onboarding, and no one actively observing how that adaptation is going, the person can enter a silent state of confusion.

They may continue working, but without really knowing whether they are doing well, whether they understood what is expected, or whether they are fitting in the way the company hoped.

Many companies treat onboarding as if it only means introducing the team, showing a few rules, and delivering access. But retention in the first months depends on much more than that. A new employee needs to feel that there is direction, support, clarity of expectations, and real follow-up.

When that does not exist, small discomforts begin to grow: insecurity about performance, difficulty asking for help, a sense of abandonment, accumulated doubts, slow adaptation to the team, and frustration from not fully understanding the role they are supposed to perform.

Another issue is that, without follow-up, the company misses the chance to notice early warning signs. Sometimes the employee is already demotivated, struggling to adapt, or failing to understand an important part of the job, but nobody notices because there is no structured process of closer observation during those first months. When the issue becomes visible, it is already more advanced: the employee is already disconnected, leadership is already dissatisfied, or departure has already become a real possibility.

## Why this is difficult
The difficult part is that early retention problems are often subtle before they become expensive.

A new employee may look active on the surface while still feeling lost underneath. Without structured checkpoints, documented expectations, and visible adaptation signals, problems stay invisible until they are much harder to recover from.

## Why it matters
This creates financial, operational, and cultural cost.

It is expensive to hire and lose someone quickly. The position becomes open again, the team absorbs the impact, and repeated early turnover creates the feeling that the company does not know how to welcome, develop, or retain people consistently.

The real problem is not only the resignation itself. It is the empty space of follow-up at the beginning of the employee journey.

## Project opportunity
This is a strong Project problem because it reflects a very common gap in HR operations: companies hire people, but do not always sustain their first months with enough structure, visibility, and support.

A useful solution here could help teams build onboarding follow-up routines, track adaptation checkpoints, surface early warning signals, and create more reliable first-month support. It is also a strong portfolio problem because it combines workflows, people operations, milestones, feedback loops, reminders, and health indicators for employee experience.

## Technical requirements
- Support onboarding plans with milestones by week or month
- Track role expectations, manager check-ins, training completion, and adaptation notes
- Allow structured follow-up between HR, manager, and employee
- Keep a timeline of onboarding events, conversations, and concerns
- Support recurring check-ins during the first 30, 60, and 90 days
- Surface incomplete onboarding steps, missed follow-ups, or unresolved blockers
- Allow employees to record questions, confidence level, or adaptation feedback
- Support manager-side evaluation of clarity, autonomy, engagement, and progress
- Keep a history of early warning signs and intervention actions
- Provide dashboards for new hires, pending follow-ups, and retention-risk indicators
- Support role-specific onboarding templates
- Work well even for companies with simple HR structures

## Suggested MVP
- New-hire onboarding tracker
- 30/60/90-day check-in flow
- Task and milestone checklist
- Manager and employee follow-up notes
- Early-risk signal dashboard
- HR overview of pending onboarding actions

## Possible extensions
- Pulse surveys for new hires
- Retention-risk scoring
- Team adaptation analytics
- Integration with HRIS or communication tools
- Automated reminder workflows
- AI-assisted summaries of onboarding progress
